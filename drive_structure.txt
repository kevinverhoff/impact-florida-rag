Impact Florida Hackweek Team Guide
About Impact Florida 
Impact Florida is a nonprofit organization that partners with school districts across the state to
improve teaching and learning through high-quality instructional materials, professional
learning, and system-level supports. We work across multiple initiatives and districts
simultaneously, supporting implementation and continuous improvement through ongoing
engagement such as district convenings, site visits, and coaching. Since 2019, we’ve worked across 70% of Florida school districts which serve 93% of the state’s student population.
What we do 
Impact Florida’s Theory of Action is designed to cultivate the conditions that support great teaching. Specifically, Impact Florida works to advance educator practice, connect education stakeholders, and influence systems to create the conditions for great teaching in every Florida classroom:
  

1. Advance Educator Practice: We build the capacity of teachers, school leaders, and systems leaders to work as a coherent team delivering excellent, engaging learning experiences for every student. Through time-bound, district cohorts organized around a shared problem of practice—called “cadres”—educators learn from one another, test new strategies, and bring lasting improvements into classrooms.  
2. Serve as a Strategic Connector: We forge strategic connections among educators, policymakers, and partners to exchange ideas, share best practices, and foster networks that support continuous learning.
3. Influence Systems: We shift mindsets, policies, and resource flows at the district and state levels—using evidence from real Florida classrooms—to create the conditions for great teaching to thrive and scale.
Currently active programs and initiatives are listed in Table 1 below. Check out our website for more information about our work, programs, and impact across Florida. 


Table 1. Active programs and initiatives 
Program/Initiative 
	Description
	  
Solving with Students Cadre
	Launched in 2021, Solving with Students is Impact Florida’s first teacher-only learning cadre. It empowers secondary math teachers to gather and act on student feedback through cycles of reflection, strategy testing, and continuous improvement. Teachers use an anonymous survey tool (PERTS) to capture student perspectives, analyze results, discuss them with students, and adjust instruction accordingly, all while receiving coaching and support from coaches and peers.
	  
Teacher Workforce Cadre
	Launched in 2023, the Teacher Workforce Cadre helps districts address teacher shortages by strengthening both recruitment and retention systems. Participants analyze data, identify root causes of attrition, and pilot new ideas—ranging from improved induction supports to districtwide retention strategies—that can scale across Florida.
	  
 Focus K-3 Math Cadre
	Launched in 2025 as the next iteration of our K–9 Focus cadre, the K–3 Focus Cadre supports districts to plan, prototype, and scale improvements in early math instruction. Teams receive customized coaching, take part in role-alike collaboration, conduct site visits and classroom observations, and use real-time data to guide decisions.
	  

Math Materials Cadre
	Launched in 2025, the Math Materials Cadre helps districts reimagine curriculum adoption as an ongoing foundation for instructional improvement, not a one-time event. The cadre builds understanding of high-quality instructional materials (HQIM), facilitates collaborative learning, and supports teams in developing locally relevant criteria for selecting materials aligned with their goals for math teaching and learning. District teams receive customized support, participate in reflective classroom walks and virtual learning sessions, and engage in implementation planning that brings teachers and leaders together to strengthen instruction systemwide.
	  
Supporting Practical AI for Real K-12 Classrooms (SPARK) 
	SPARK is an 18-month initiative designed to help Florida school districts use artificial intelligence in thoughtful, responsible, and practical ways. The initiative is led by Impact Florida in partnership with the University of Florida’s CSEveryone Center for Computer Science Education, with alignment to the Florida K-12 AI Education Task Force and support from partners including Microsoft, Google, and other key stakeholders across the state. Note: SPARK has recently launched and has just completed the district recruitment phase. 
	  

NAT HQIM/Policy & Advocacy 
Not on website
	Launched in 2026, this HQIM policy initiative supports Florida’s efforts to strengthen policies and systems that increase access to high-quality instructional materials (HQIM). In addition to continued work supporting districts through instructional materials learning and adoption efforts, the initiative focuses on three areas: (1) advising state leaders and the Florida Department of Education on instructional materials review, evaluation, and adoption processes; (2) researching and documenting policy, funding, procurement, and implementation barriers that limit HQIM adoption and effective use; and (3) engaging policymakers, education leaders, and advocacy partners with evidence-based recommendations that strengthen instructional materials systems and support high-quality math instruction statewide. This initiative aims to create the conditions for more informed instructional materials decisions and stronger learning experiences for students across Florida.
	  
EIR/Game-Based Learning
Not on website  
	Launched in 2024 through a five-year Education Innovation and Research (EIR) grant from the U.S. Department of Education, Game-Based Learning Platform to Enhance Student Science is a partnership among Impact Florida, Legends of Learning, and WestEd to improve and evaluate the use of game-based learning in 5th-grade science classrooms in Miami-Dade County Public Schools using Legends of Learning, a specific game-based learning product. Impact Florida leads district engagement, while Legends of Learning provides the software and WestEd leads the independent research program that includes usability and feasibility studies, a yearlong implementation study, and a large-scale randomized controlled trial to examine the impact of game-based learning on student engagement, opportunities to learn, and science achievement. Note: The project team is currently preparing to launch the impact RCT study in Fall 2026.
	  


Florida Materials Navigator 
www.flmaterialsnavigator.org
	Launching in July 2026, the Florida Materials Navigator will help district leaders make informed instructional materials decisions by providing practical, Florida-specific resources aligned to the state’s adoption process. The Navigator curates guidance, tools, templates, and learning opportunities that support districts in selecting and implementing high-quality instructional materials. Developed in partnership with state and national organizations, the Navigator combines evidence-based frameworks with local context to help adoption teams evaluate materials, plan for implementation, and learn from the experiences of districts across Florida. Note: The Florida Materials Navigator is currently under development. 
	

Hackweek Project Description and Deliverables 
Using AI to Identify Patterns and Insights Across Impact Florida’s Professional Learning Initiatives
Impact Florida’s Need
As part of our work at Impact Florida, we generate regular (typically monthly) summaries and a variety of other artifacts (e.g., decks, workpapers, transcripts from virtual or in-person meetings, chart papers, etc.) for each project that synthesize progress, lessons learned, and insights from district engagement. These summaries and artifacts represent a rich but currently under-leveraged source of qualitative data about implementation, educator experience, and emerging trends across districts and programs.


Your charge during the Impact Florida Hackweek is to develop a prototype AI/LLM-based tool that can analyze these monthly summaries and additional artifacts to identify patterns,
themes, and insights across districts and initiatives and support our internal strategic learning and progress toward our outcome targets. The tool would enable cross-project synthesis that is currently time-intensive and largely manual.

In addition to monthly summaries and additional artifacts, we also collect quantitative data through program surveys and website analytics. Ideally (if feasible), we would be interested in a model capable of analyzing both qualitative and quantitative data from several sources.
What You’ll Do During Hackweek

During Hackweek, you’ll work with Impact Florida to: 
* Review and understand the structure and content of existing monthly summaries and related materials (and, if feasible, additional qualitative and quantitative data sources).
* Define a clear approach for analyzing these documents/data sources using AI/ML techniques.
* Develop a prototype tool or workflow that can ingest multiple summaries/data sources and extract key themes and patterns.
* Explore approaches for structuring outputs in a way that is useful for organizational learning and decision-making.
The scope is focused on developing a functional prototype and demonstrating feasibility, rather
than building a fully production-ready system. In addition to monthly summaries, Impact
Florida has a range of related artifacts and other quantitative data that are currently
underutilized, and we would be open to discussing other potential approaches or project
directions that could help the organization identify patterns and insights to inform strategic
planning and organizational learning.
________________
Proposed Outcomes & Deliverables
We are seeking a prototype tool or workflow that enables Impact Florida to more
systematically extract and synthesize insights across our work.


Desired outcomes include:


* A working prototype (e.g., script, interface, or workflow) that can process multiple monthly summaries and additional artifacts (and, if feasible, additional qualitative and quantitative data sources) and identify recurring themes and patterns.
* The ability to query the model with questions (e.g., “What challenges are most frequently emerging across districts?”) and receive synthesized responses grounded in the source materials.
* Outputs that include not only identified themes but also contextual information such as frequency, timing (when themes emerge), and whether patterns are concentrated within specific districts or appear across multiple districts/programs.
* Documentation of the approach, including how data are structured, how the model processes inputs, and how outputs are generated
* A demonstration or example analysis using a subset of Impact Florida materials


In addition, we would like to explore potential formats for short, strategic learning memos
generated from the analysis. These would be concise (1–2 page) summaries based on strategic learning questions asked of the model, including relevant context, high-level themes, and key takeaways. The goal is to produce internal outputs that can be readily used by senior leadership to inform ongoing organizational learning and decision-making (and may inform future public-facing deliverables, following staff review).
Example Verification Questions
The verification questions below are intended as example benchmarks for assessing whether the prototype is functioning as intended. They are not meant to define the full scope of the model's capabilities. Ideally, the prototype should be designed to ingest and synthesize insights across the diverse data sources and artifacts provided, enabling users to explore a broad range of strategic learning, evaluation, and program improvement questions.
To assess whether the AI prototype is operating as intended, we may query the model with questions such as:
1. What participant experiences are most consistently valued across Impact Florida's programs, and which appear most connected to meaningful changes in educator practice, collaboration, or systems improvement?
2. How have the most common challenges facing districts and educators changed over time across Impact Florida initiatives, and what do these patterns suggest about opportunities for future programming, support structures, and strategic investments?

Additional Considerations & Areas for Exploration 
Please include: Output Disclaimer
All outputs generated by the prototype should include a clear reminder that they are derived from internal organizational data and should be treated as confidential. Include something such as the following (using red text if possible). 
Disclaimer: This output is generated from internal data and should be treated as confidential; please review and remove any sensitive information before sharing externally.
Ideal: Future Historical Data & Artifact Ingestion
Ideally, the prototype would be designed with flexibility to accommodate additional historical information sources in the future. For this initial prototype, materials from several past initiatives (e.g., Opportunity Cadre, You+Lead, Focus Cadre, and others) were intentionally excluded. These materials may include structured datasets as well as unstructured artifacts such as reports, resources, participant submissions, program documentation, communications, and other records generated through prior programmatic efforts.
The system architecture should support the future incorporation of these diverse information sources if the organization chooses to expand the model’s knowledge base. Fellows should consider how the prototype can be designed to efficiently ingest, organize, and leverage both structured and unstructured content while maintaining flexibility as new sources are added over time. If not feasible during Hackweek, understanding the technical feasibility and the level of effort that would be required to incorporate this into the system architecture would be helpful.  
If feasibility is a concern: Potential Scope Narrowing
As discussed during the project kickoff, if the team determines that the current scope is too broad for the available timeline, we recommend focusing on SWS and Focus K-3 programs. These initiatives have the most comprehensive collection of available data and artifacts, while also representing sufficiently different program models and participant experiences. As a result, they may provide a strong foundation for developing and testing an approach that could later be generalized to other Impact Florida programs. 
If the team opts to go this route, please discuss with Jenn and share more information about the feasibility of and level of effort that would be needed to incorporate all data sources per the original proposal. 
If time allows: Exploration of HubSpot-ChatGPT Web Analytics Data Integration
Impact Florida collects substantial communications and engagement data through HubSpot, its CRM and marketing platform. Due to the volume of available web analytics data and HubSpot’s existing reporting and dashboarding capabilities, these data sources were not listed in our data sources table. Fellows are encouraged to explore whether HubSpot web analytics data can be integrated into the prototype in a meaningful and technically feasible way. (Note: Based on our initial research, there is a HubSpot-ChatGPT connector, but it seems to be designed to pull customer records rather than website analytics; additional investigation is needed). 
Examples of available HubSpot web analytics data include: website traffic and page views; bounce rates; new versus returning visitors; form views and form submissions; resource downloads and content engagement; email open rates; email click-through rates; LinkedIn audience growth’ LinkedIn post impressions and engagement metrics. 
Fellows are encouraged to assess whether an existing HubSpot web analytics integration is available and, if so, whether it can be easily incorporated to the prototype. If not, consider what level of effort would be required to incorporate this into the system architecture.
